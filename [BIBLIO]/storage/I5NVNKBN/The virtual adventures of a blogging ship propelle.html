The virtual adventures of a blogging ship propelled by weather data

By Robert Barry
05 February 13

For the last year, a virtual ship has been adrift in the world, being pushed around by weather data generated in London, composing a logbased on geo-tagged information it finds online. As the project comes to a close, Wired.co.uk speaks to the project's creator, writer, artist and technologist James Bridle

"I am lost." Those three words marked the last transmission from the Roi des Belges, one afternoon in January 2013. Not the real Roi des Belges, the little steamboat that Joseph Conrad sailed up the Congo in the summer of 1890, nor its fictionalised twin in Heart of Darkness, but a virtual instantiation of a reproduction of Conrad's ship, perched on top of the Southbank Centre and drifting through cyberspace.

This Roi des Belges traversed half the world in 365 days without once leaving London. It was a boutique one-room hotel -- a "Room for London" -- an architectural project inspired by Conrad's novella. It was designed as a place "of refuge and reflection"; a temporary home for artistic interventions and live concerts; and a project -- by James Bridle, the former publisher turned curator of the New Aesthetic -- about "ships, weather, and the internet".

"It was a very open brief," Bridle explained to me of his role in the project. "I was initially asked to provide some kind of link between the physical installation and the internet." He pursued this link by building another boat, a little brother to the replica Roi (designed by David Kohn Architects and Fiona Banner), which served as a weather station, gathering sensor data from the site and posting it to the Room for London webpage.

Unsatisfied with a boat that just sat there on top of the National Theatre, "I wanted to make the ship move, and I wanted to make it speak," wrote Bridle on the blogpost that launched the project, "and I wanted to speak back to it, with it, together." So if the weather station picked up a five miles-per-hour westerly wind, Bridle would plot a course east for five miles, sending the ship adrift on a virtual journey across the globe. As if fated, the ship's first port of call was Poland, Conrad's home and heartland.

As the ship drifted, it scanned its surroundings for Wikipedia edits, FourSquare check-ins, Tweets, Flickr photos, "anything that's available online that's occasionally reliably geo-tagged." Whatever it found, it tried to communicate with; reading any attached text and "doing some fairly basic language generation stuff using Markov chains," which is to say, applying the same methods as spam generators and content farms; the native fauna of the internet. The results became the ship's log and Twitter feed.

"I'm not a terribly good programmer," Bridle admits, but he found his simple code allowed him to leave the boat pretty autonomous once set on its way. The only major change in course came in the spring when the ship was banned from scraping content from Grindr, the geosocial networking app aimed at gay men. "That was quite a core input to it," says Bridle, "which related it quite closely to some of my readings of Conrad." Ousted on suspicion of compromising Grindr's security protocols, Bridle supplemented the ship's reading with extracts from Conrad and other short stories.

Bridle first came to the attention of Wired readers in 2010, when he published the complete changelogs of Wikipedia's page on the Iraq War as a deluxe twelve-volume set of hardback books (A Ship Adrift's log is slated for physical publication "hopefully in the next month or so"). These days he's more interested in the bots which make up two-thirds of Wikipedia's most frequent editors, and in "the quality of language of spam and the kind of broken text you find on the internet," which he calls "a vernacular of the network itself."

He uses the phrase " networked fiction" to describe both "fictions that connect to other kinds of fiction" like fan fiction; and those fictions "revealed by certain writers having a particular viewpoint across the network," like the recent novels of William Gibson, which make our present feel like science fiction by virtue of the hyper-connectivity of their author. The phrase is "an attempt to articulate what it feels like to live in a time when very distant things are made visible and immediate by the network." What, the phrase asks, does the net bring to storytelling that is new?

"We've known for twenty years that computers are now better than humans at chess," Bridle says by way of analogy, but "it turns out that the best computer can be beaten by a team made up of a human and a computer. And you can see [similar things] happening across lots of different areas, from marketing to poetry." His one direct intervention in the project was to order the last few tweets to give a sense of closure. That sense of an ending -- what Frank Kermode once called the elementary gesture of literary form -- remains elusive to the automated. But Bridle finds in the disjointed prose of his algorithmic airship some sense of an "initial encounter with the world and a desperate attempt to process it."

There remains one thing Bridle will admit he still hasn't figured out about the project. "At what point do we admit that these systems have something resembling agency or intelligence? Because obviously if essentially a randomly generated co-ordinate on the Earth's surface links to three Wikipedia articles and then an automated program spits out three words on Twitter, that's not intelligence. And yet it has the potential to create incredibly strong emotional experiences in us. That for me stands in microcosm for all of our interactions with technology and the possibilities that are inherent in them."
