Study: brainless robots swarm just like animals

By David Cornish
15 March 13
Robots

The future replacement for the human race and frequent asset to humanity

Swarming patterns and herding behaviours have been observed throughout the animal kingdom. Scientists and mathematicians have pondered the cause of complex relationships and group dynamics at work that allow schools of fish, such as herring, and flocks of birds, such as starlings, to move together in apparent unity -- and now, in an interesting twist to the discussion, a team of engineers from Harvard University has observed apparent collective behaviour in brainless robots.

The robot research team was looking for a way to investigate the transition that swarming groups make from random behaviour into collective motion. In order to observe a randomly moving collective, they built the simplest of "self-propelled automatons", the charmingly named Bristle-Bot (BBots).

A single bot consists of a small eccentric motor, commonly used to supply vibrate functions in mobile phones, two sets of toothbrush bristles, a rechargeable battery and an elliptical plastic chassis. The toothbrush bristles are held in place under the plastic chassis by an angled wedge, acting as the bots legs. When the motor is activated, the vibrations cause the weight of the robot to be displaced, and the bot moves in the direction the bristles are angled. The team created two sets of BBots; one with longer bristles, that caused the BBots to move in straight lines, and one with shorter bristles, that resulted in them moving in a circular motion.

The spinning BBots would collide frequently in tests, often clustering close to the edge of the circular test area they were confined to, while "walking" BBots would move from one side of the boundary to the other.

To study group behaviour, the research team gradually increased the number of bots in a confined circular study area 44cm across. A small number of spinning BBots would result in random movement, occasionally forming a bunch that would quickly break up. However, when a threshold number was reached, they became a "self organised" group that moved coherently around the edge of their confinement. The same behaviour occurred with walkers, which would only group together after a certain critical mass was achieved.

The significant claim of the study is in its challenge that density-driven transitions in biological swarm behaviour might result from something more than social interactions and animal cognition. "Our study suggests that particle motion, shape and spatial interactions are sufficient and might in fact play equivalent roles," write the paper's authors. "In a biological setting such as termite swarms, one might test these ideas by controlling the confinement of termites by varying the substrate curvature and slipperiness, gluing circular discs on their backs to make the interactions more isotropic."

Further tests will have to be carried out to determine if mechanical functions of groups play a significant role in cognitive species. Until then, check out the above video to see the little BristleBots in action, and pray the robot swarm doesn't become any more sinister than it already sounds.
