How to make balloon maps

By Madhumita Venkataramanan
22 March 13

To make precise digital maps, you just need a balloon and a smartphone, says Stewart Long of Boston's Public Laboratory for Open Technology and Science, which promotes "making open-source tools to monitor our environment". So how about helping to build the world's most detailed public aerial map?

1. Build it
You'll need a helium balloon no longer than 1.8 metres as well as a private balloon licence, 1.1 metres cubed of helium, 300 metres of string, a yo-yo reel and a camera. The camera can be a smartphone or a basic point-and-shoot, set to continuous mode, which you put in a plastic bottle for protection and secure to the balloon string.

2. Scale it
You need to decide on the scale of your map. "Your altitude is your image resolution, so height is key," says Long. "Ninety per cent of our flights are within 500 metres and most are below 100." Consider your target mapping area: closer to the ground makes for small, precise maps and higher up gives greater range but lower resolution.

3. Launch it
For the best images you need to make sure it's a bright and still day. You're also looking for obstacles such as houses, power lines, trees and nearby airports. "Let the balloon up 20 to 30 metres vertically, and see if there are any problems you can run into," Long says. "Then keep letting
it go higher to the altitude you want."

4. Digitise it
Once your balloon's grounded, the images need to be stitched into a map. "We built mapknitter.org, which will present a default Google Maps view," says Long. Using the "new image" tool, you can fit the images to the map. "It will then make a big geotiff image file, which is a standardised format in geographic information systems."

5. Publish it
Any finished maps can be published on the Public Lab's website, annotated with geographic metadata and archived. "These maps are all in the public domain and eventually will be part of Open-AerialMap, a companion to the existing OpenStreetMap," says Long. Visit publiclaboratory.org to find out more.
