Designer publishes collection of developer-written 'code poems'

By Olivia Solon
12 October 12

View Gallery

Related features

  • Creative project invites developers to write 'code poems'
  • A manifesto for your art manifesto
  • What tech couldn't you live without?
  • Should kids grow up without real books?
Comments 1

In February 2012, Ishac Bertran launched a project inviting people to submit "code poems" -- poetry written in any coding language. After receiving around 200 such poems from 30 countries, he has selected 55 of them and created a book, called code {poems}.

The poems are written in a variety of programming languages, including C++, HTML, C#, SQL, Objective C, Applescript and Java. They cover a range of topics including love, science, news, religion, maths, life, robots and poetry. There were two rules that submissions had to follow: they had to have a maximum size of 0.5 KB and they had to compile.

The resulting book has a a foreword written by Jamie Allen, the head of research at the Copenhagen Institute of Interaction Design, and a limited print run of 100, each numbered. The book was compiled and edited by Allen, along with David Gauthier, Joshua Noble and Marcin Ignac.

Bertran told Wired.co.uk: "From comments I received from the authors it seems they felt like defining the boundaries of code poetry while creating their poem."

According to Bertran, the biggest challenge was making the book feel natural in people's hands. "It's been really exciting to design a book in which the code feels comfortable living in this physical medium, and poetry doesn't feel betrayed. Code literature might still sound strange but it feels right to me, to have around in our times."

Bertran is planning an exhibition about the project, with visual interpretations by different artists.
